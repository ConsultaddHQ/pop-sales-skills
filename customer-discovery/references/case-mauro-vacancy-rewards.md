# Reference Case: Mauro Rodriguez — Vacancy Rewards

This is a second canonical case for /customer-discovery, representing a DIFFERENT ICP than the Brian Redtfeldt case. Brian is a solo operator in Pop's sweet spot ($215-$495/mo). Mauro is a B2B tech buyer at an established company with internal dev resources — OUTSIDE the sweet spot, different sale, different offering.

## What Manushree's (SDR) manual brief had

- Structured prospect profile: name, role, email, LinkedIn, meeting time
- Company context: HubSpot launched late 2025, moving away from proprietary tools
- Two-call history: April 6 insights captured (ROI focus, strategic pivot, wants assistance for current process)
- AI maturity: Medium-High, technical literacy Very High
- Qualification assessment: High, AI Ready, Stage Exploratory/Demo

**What Manushree did better than Aditi:** Captured prior call notes, flagged technical maturity correctly, identified the strategic pivot in language ("critical configuration" over new builds), framed the sales stage.

**What this brief missed:**

1. **Vacancy Rewards business model.** "Travel & Hospitality / Vacation Tech" is too vague. It's a vacation ownership / timeshare rewards operation. The customer base is members who trade points for stays. Different pain profile than OTA or hotel tech.

2. **Mauro's tenure and authority.** "20+ years in development" doesn't tell us if he's been at Vacancy Rewards for 20 years (likely high authority, institutional memory) or joined recently (likely hired to drive the HubSpot migration). LinkedIn would clarify.

3. **Pop ICP mismatch.** Pop's Starter ($215) and Growth ($495) plans are tuned for solo operators and small businesses with no internal dev capacity. Mauro runs a dev team actively configuring APIs. **The retail plans are irrelevant to this deal.** The brief should flag this explicitly and propose different offering framing.

4. **Vulnerability signal specificity.** "Launched HubSpot late 2025" is a fact. The signal is: he's 4-6 months in and needs to show ROI by next budget cycle (Q3/Q4). If he can't demonstrate the investment paid off, his budget next year shrinks.

5. **The hidden differentiator.** Mauro's specific ask — "critical configuration" over "building from zero" — is the tell. He's looking to **buy outcomes, not tooling**. Task-specific pre-built agents that plug into HubSpot fit his mental model exactly. The brief doesn't name this explicitly.

6. **Decision process.** The brief says "Qualification: High" but doesn't clarify whether Mauro can sign the contract or whether he needs CFO / CEO approval. This matters for sizing next steps.

## Hidden Differentiator (the thing nobody else is telling them)

**Mauro isn't buying tools. He's buying the end of the "build vs. buy" debate.**

His language gives it away: "critical configuration" over "starting new projects from zero." He's explicitly tired of the maintenance cost and obsolescence of homegrown systems. He wants someone else to own the code so his team can own the outcomes. Pre-built agents that plug into HubSpot match that mental model perfectly — they're NOT new tools, they're extensions to what he's already chosen.

**This is a different sale than Pop's usual.** Don't pitch content engines or one-pagers. Pitch an agent library where he pays per workflow, and each agent is a pre-built pattern deployed in under a day.

## Vulnerability Signals (why now)

- **HubSpot ROI clock is running.** Launched late 2025, 4-6 months in. Next budget cycle will evaluate whether the investment paid off. He needs wins fast.
- **Internal dev costs are hurting.** Moved away from proprietary tools specifically because of "high maintenance costs and rapid obsolescence." That's a finance pain, not just an engineering one.
- **Strategic pivot at the team level.** The team's direction is shifting from build to integrate. Any external tool that supports that pivot will get internal momentum behind it.

**5 Whys applied:**
- Surface: "We want an AI layer on top of existing stack."
- Why? They just invested in HubSpot and don't want to re-platform.
- Why does that matter? Re-platforming is expensive, and HubSpot was the decision already made.
- Why does that matter? The whole team has aligned on HubSpot; internal politics will resist another shift.
- Why does that matter? Mauro's job is to make HubSpot work, not to justify another tool.
- **Core:** Mauro is accountable for HubSpot's ROI. Anything that makes HubSpot more valuable is a win for him personally. Anything that suggests HubSpot was the wrong choice is politically toxic.

**Sell this:** "We make HubSpot more valuable." Don't sell "we replace parts of HubSpot."
