# Reference Case: Scott Baker — Mister Mac

This is the third canonical case. Brian is a solo operator, sweet-spot ICP. Mauro is a B2B tech buyer outside ICP. Scott is a DIFFERENT thing again: a **technical founder who pivoted from customer to partner**, with a multi-meeting deal history spanning 2+ months.

**What this case teaches:** `/customer-discovery` alone is not enough. The skill system needs a separate `/meeting-prep` skill that handles ongoing deals with full history, evolving ask, and shifting relationship shape. Discovery is for meeting 1. Prep is for meetings 2+.

## What the original SDR brief (Sumit's) had

- Prospect: Scott Baker, Owner of Mister Mac, Austin TX
- Company: Small Apple device repair/support/education company, founded 2018
- Initial positioning: "already use AI tools, capable of building many solutions internally, may need a specific piece of AI software"
- Generic workflow automation NOT a priority
- Link to Trellus call transcript

**What Sumit got right:** Good prospect profile basics, captured the "we can build ourselves" signal, logged call transcripts systematically across 5 meetings.

**What this data point reveals about SDR research gaps:**

The initial SDR research didn't catch:
1. **Scott's ex-Apple background.** This is the source of his customer-service philosophy. It explains why his business works and why he can't easily train junior techs.
2. **Scott's customer demographic (elderly).** This is his ICP, and it explains why he refuses AI-at-the-phone. Changes the entire pitch.
3. **Scott's technical ability.** He builds his own AI tools. He's not the "doesn't know what AI is" prospect. He evaluates engineering.
4. **Scott's business model constraint.** Solo technician, can't scale without training, training is hard because soft skills + Apple knowledge is a rare combo.

A proper `/customer-discovery` brief would have flagged all four on day one. That might have prevented the initial $5-8k proposal mismatch.

# Discovery Brief: Scott Baker — Mister Mac (Austin)

**Generated by:** /customer-discovery
**Date:** 2026-02-17 (day before first call)
**For AE:** Ankit
**Prepared by:** Sumit

## TL;DR

Scott is a technical founder running Mister Mac, a 7-year-old Apple device support business in Austin. He's ex-Apple, 39, and his competitive moat is **elderly customers who explicitly reject AI-at-the-phone** ("they don't want to talk to a machine"). His business model is capped because HE is the only technician — training junior techs is the bottleneck, but training requires both Apple technical knowledge AND refined soft skills (yes sir / yes ma'am communication). He is NOT a "teach them about AI" buyer — he builds his own AI tools, evaluates engineering directly. **Do NOT pitch customer-facing AI; that destroys his moat.** The real opening: AI that coaches junior technicians DURING calls, so Scott can scale the technician role without sacrificing customer experience. Start with understanding the technician training bottleneck before pitching anything. Budget range is likely $1k-10k for the right solution, but he'll push back hard on anything that looks overpriced or under-engineered.

- **ICP:** Elderly customers and families who value human contact over automated systems

- **Background:** 39 years old. Ex-Apple employee. Built Mister Mac in 2018. Has built his own internal AI tool for QuickBooks workflow (charges $19 to customers for it).
- **LinkedIn:** https://www.linkedin.com/in/scottmbaker3/

- **AI posture:** **HIGH and sophisticated.** Already builds AI tools. Already using AI for internal workflow. Evaluates on engineering substance. NOT a hype prospect, NOT a novice. Will smell BS immediately.

## Hidden Differentiator (the thing nobody else is telling them)

**Scott's moat is explicitly human-first service for customers who reject AI. His personal brand — "ex-Apple, 7 years doing this, in-between the postmodern era" — is the product.**

Direct quote: "I have a very personal one on one service... teaching the elderly how to use their iPhone. Who will never catch up to AI and they don't ever wanna talk to a machine. You know? They they like that they call, and I have someone pick up the phone, and there's not a whole bunch of 'press one for this.' That's what's made my my business successful."

**This is the insight everyone will miss:** Any pitch that involves customer-facing AI (voice agents, chatbots, automated scheduling) DESTROYS Scott's moat. Don't go there. The only AI he wants is the kind that **makes his human technicians better while remaining invisible to customers.**

## Vulnerability Signals (why now)

- **Scaling is blocked by training cost.** Scott is the only technician. His business cannot grow revenue without adding technicians. Training technicians requires both Apple knowledge (kids have it) AND communication maturity (older folks have it). These rarely combine.
- **One trainer leaving / hiring friction.** Hiring and training cycle is months-long. Losing any tech-capable staff creates immediate capacity crisis.
- **Competitive AI pressure.** Apple Store, Best Buy Geek Squad, local competitors — all getting more automated. Scott's human-first moat holds IF he can scale the human operation. If he can't, the moat narrows.

**5 Whys applied:**
- Surface: "I can't find good technicians."
- Why? Kids know Apple but not communication. Adults know communication but not Apple.
- Why does that matter? Scott has to do all the technical work personally.
- Why does that matter? His business is capped at 1 technician's output.
- Why does that matter? Revenue growth requires capacity growth.
- **Core:** Scott cannot grow his business because the human-first moat that makes him valuable also makes him impossible to clone. AI coaching junior technicians in real-time solves this without compromising the moat.

**Do NOT pitch:** customer-facing voice agents, chatbots, automated scheduling, generic content marketing, or anything that replaces human touch.

## Discovery Questions for the Call

Each grounded in research:

1. "I noticed you were at Apple before Mister Mac. How did that shape how you run the business today?"
2. "Your customer base skews older from what I can tell. How do they feel about AI generally? Has that changed?"
3. "You built a QuickBooks tool yourself. What did that teach you about what AI can and can't do in your workflow?"
4. "You're the only technician right now. What's the #1 thing preventing you from training a junior technician who can deliver at your level?"
5. "If you could watch every screen-share call your junior tech did and whisper the right Apple-technical answer to them in real time, what would that unlock?"
6. "What have you tried on the hiring/training side? What worked, what didn't?"

## What this case teaches the skill system (beyond /customer-discovery)

**`/customer-discovery` handles meeting 1.** Produces the brief above.

**`/meeting-prep` is a different skill** needed for meetings 2+. It needs to:

1. **Read all prior meeting notes** (discovery, demo, follow-up, proposal review)
2. **Track deal shape changes** — Scott went from $5-8k customer → $1k POC → partnership with rev share
3. **Flag commitments made** — e.g., "Bharat committed to send screen-monitoring video via email," "Next meeting May 5 at 1 PM CT"
4. **Identify what's still open** — partnership structure (60/40 vs 55/45), screen analysis technical feasibility, Scott's product clarity timeline (4-5 weeks)
5. **Surface vulnerability shifts** — Scott is 4-5 weeks from his own product clarity. That timing window matters for Pop's follow-up cadence.
6. **Prep the specific next meeting** — May 5, 1 PM CT, after Scott has seen the screen-monitoring video Bharat sends

**`/meeting-debrief` is a third skill** that runs AFTER each call and:
1. Captures new information (Scott built a QuickBooks tool for $19/pop)
2. Updates the customer profile
3. Logs commitments made by both sides
4. Identifies what changed in the deal shape
5. Drafts the follow-up email

## The real lesson from Scott's case

**The original pitch failed** ($5-8k proposal rejected) because the initial research missed the moat insight. If `/customer-discovery` had flagged "DO NOT pitch customer-facing AI" on day one, Ankit would have led with technician-facing AI copilot from the start, avoided the first rejection, and reached the partnership conversation faster.

**The deal was saved** because Bharat found the partnership framing. But that took 2 extra meetings. A sharper discovery would have saved that time.
