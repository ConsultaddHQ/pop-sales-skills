# Hiring-Inflection Signals (Reference)

When these appear in a call, the deal is gated until a new team arrives AND the tools those hires bring become your default competition. Always apply the pre-audit counter-move.

## Trigger signals to listen for

**Team arrival timing:**
- "Starting May 1st" / "begins next month" / "onboarding in June"
- "New team joining" / "just hired" / "onboarding"
- "Once they're ready" / "when they get on board"
- "Next month when they begin"

**Deference to incoming team:**
- "I want their opinion" / "I'll loop them in"
- "I'll have to ask them" / "they'll evaluate this"
- "They're the ones doing it" / "I trust their judgment"

**Tool-conflict risk:**
- "They have tools they'll be using"
- "They mentioned some existing options"
- "They come with their own stack"
- "They're bringing their preferred tools"

## Counter-move: Pre-audit

When 2+ of these signals appear in a single call, `/meeting-debrief` should flag the deal as HIRING-INFLECTION and the follow-up email should include the pre-audit offer.

### Pre-audit template (adapt per prospect)

```
Between now and {team start date}, we can run a {5-7} day audit on
{relevant domain for this prospect — ranking, workflow efficiency,
customer churn, etc.}. We deliver the output 1-2 days before your
new team arrives.

Why: your new hires land to a document with real data about your
business, not a vendor pitch deck. You become the advisor they
turn to, not the vendor they evaluate.

Cost: we absorb the audit as part of getting to know your business.
No commitment beyond letting us share the findings.
```

### What to ask

At the end of the debrief, always ask the prospect:
- "Who on the new team should be on our next call?"
- "What tools are your new hires planning to use? No trash-talk — just want to come prepared."

Learning the competition for free is worth the awkwardness of asking.

## Why this matters

The default behavior is to wait for the team to start. Waiting = losing. The tools the new hires bring have inertia:

- They already know how to use them
- They trust the vendor relationship
- Evaluating "your tool vs. mine" feels like a political battle internally
- The new hire's first 30 days are about proving they know what they're doing

You can't win in that frame. The pre-audit flips the frame: now the new hires walk in to find data about their own business, and the conversation becomes "how do we use this audit to do our job better?" You're the advisor, not the vendor.
